# Argument and Terminology Contract

Use this gate after the answerable-unanswered-question check and before drafting full prose. The goal is to lock the manuscript's central argument, reader promise, paragraph jobs, and canonical terminology before the draft becomes fluent but unstable.

## Core Principle

A deliverable manuscript needs one bounded argument. If the one-sentence argument cannot be written without inventing evidence, the manuscript direction is not ready for full drafting.

## One-Sentence Argument

Use this pattern, then make it field-specific and cautious:

```text
In [system / conservation problem], this study [describes / evaluates / estimates / identifies] [bounded advance or pattern] using [data and method], supported by [key evidence], with conclusions limited to [claim boundary].
```

For observational wildlife monitoring data, avoid "we show" or "we demonstrate" unless the design and analysis provide strong direct evidence. Prefer "this study evaluates", "this study identifies", "records suggest", or "patterns were consistent with".

## Reader Question Sequence

Before drafting, check that the manuscript can answer these questions in order:

1. Relevance: who should care about this system, species group, landscape, method, or management problem?
2. Contribution: what is specifically added beyond nearby literature and local reporting?
3. Trust: what data, methods, effort, diagnostics, and uncertainty make the claim believable?
4. Reuse: what can another researcher, manager, or monitoring team reuse from the dataset, method, result cards, figures, or data package?
5. Boundary: where do the conclusions stop?

If "Contribution" or "Trust" cannot be answered, downgrade the article type or add analysis/literature tasks before drafting.

## Paragraph Job Map

Each major paragraph should have one primary job:

- context
- gap
- method boundary
- definition
- objective
- result
- comparison
- explanation
- limitation
- management implication
- future data need

If a paragraph has two unrelated jobs, split it. If two adjacent paragraphs have the same job, merge or delete one unless both are necessary for different results.

## Terminology Ledger

Create a compact terminology ledger before drafting or polishing:

| Canonical term | First-use definition | Variants seen | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|
|  |  |  |  |

Include:

- species names and taxonomic authority when relevant,
- study area, protected area, zones, edge/gradient terms, and coordinate proxies,
- metrics such as RAI, detection rate, occupancy, occurrence, diversity, effort, camera days, acoustic events, or threat index,
- model names, abbreviations, packages, ordination axes, and uncertainty terms,
- management terms such as patrol, livestock, community forest, corridor, or restoration.

Rules:

- One concept gets one name across Title, Abstract, Methods, Results, Discussion, figures, and supplements.
- Define abbreviations once at first use, then use the canonical short form.
- Do not vary terminology for style. Consistency outranks lexical variety.
- If a term is a proxy, keep the proxy wording. Do not rename it as the ecological process it only approximates.
- If two terms conflict, mark `author confirmation needed` instead of silently choosing.

## Passing Standard

The gate passes when the manuscript has a defensible one-sentence argument, every central term has a canonical form, and each planned paragraph serves a clear function without hiding unsupported claims behind polished prose.
