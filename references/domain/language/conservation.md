# Conservation Language

Use management-relevant language only after the statistical result is clear.

## Translate Without Overclaiming

Weak: Road distance was significant.

Better: After accounting for elevation and forest type, sites farther from roads had higher estimated occupancy, suggesting road-associated disturbance may be relevant for spatial prioritization.

Weak: Patrols reduced threats.

Better: Threat records declined after patrol intensification, but without a matched control area the result should be interpreted as evidence consistent with improvement rather than definitive causal attribution.

Weak: The forest is intact because cameras recorded many species.

Better: Camera-trap records indicate a relatively diverse medium- and large-bodied mammal assemblage, which is one line of evidence for animal-community integrity. Vegetation, connectivity, and human-pressure indicators are still needed for a broader habitat-integrity assessment.

## Useful Conservation Outputs

- Status: occupancy, trend, density, distribution, activity, community composition.
- Threat: location, intensity, uncertainty, affected species or habitats.
- Action: patrol priority, monitoring improvement, corridor verification, habitat restoration target.
- Risk: false negatives, false positives, uncertainty, cost, accessibility.

## Management-Ready Sentence Pattern

In [area/time], [indicator] was [higher/lower/changed] in relation to [driver or condition], with [uncertainty]. This suggests [bounded interpretation]. For management, this supports [specific action or next data need], while avoiding [unsupported stronger claim].
